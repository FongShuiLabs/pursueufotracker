"Quotes Coronal Discharges": Apollo 12's Crew Explains the Lights They Saw With Their Eyes Closed

NASA-UAP-D008 is an audio recording of the Apollo 12 crew's post-mission medical debriefing. A flight surgeon opens the session by reading Commander Charles "Pete" Conrad, Command Module Pilot Richard "Dick" Gordon, and Lunar Module Pilot Alan Bean a paragraph about "quotes coronal discharges" they had reported seeing in space. What follows is nine minutes of the crew comparing notes, in granular detail, on flashes and streaks of light seen with their eyes closed in a dark cabin - and the flight surgeon connecting it to Apollo 11's Buzz Aldrin and Neil Armstrong, and to NASA's earlier, inconclusive attempts to photograph the phenomenon on the retina during Project Mercury.

What this file is

NASA-UAP-D008 is a 470-second (7 minute, 50 second) audio/video recording, sourced from NASA and released through the PURSUE archive, titled "Apollo 12 Medical Debriefing - Tape 12, 1969." Unlike the well-known Apollo 11 and Apollo 17 Technical Crew Debriefings - which cover engineering and mission-performance topics with UAP-adjacent content appearing as an aside - this is specifically a medical debriefing, conducted by a NASA flight surgeon with all three Apollo 12 crew members present: Commander Charles "Pete" Conrad, Command Module Pilot Richard F. "Dick" Gordon, and Lunar Module Pilot Alan L. Bean. The site's automated transcript (Whisper-generated, embedded on the file's own page) captures the full session as continuous conversational audio, not a formal Q&A transcript with speaker labels - so where this page attributes a specific line to Conrad, Gordon, or Bean, that attribution is based on content and context (who is described as sleeping on which side of the spacecraft, who is being addressed directly), consistent with how the astronauts refer to each other by name and role within the recording itself.

The recording opens mid-conversation, with the flight surgeon reading from a document already in front of him:

"Feel it a bit there. Uh, listen, we read this paragraph in here about these quotes coronal discharges that you guys were seeing and, and that, that completely stops us. Tell, tell us what, how this came about and who, who talked to you about it and what and what you saw."

That opening line is the entire hook of this file. "Coronal discharges" - in quotation marks, as the flight surgeon reads it - is the term someone used, in writing, to describe what the Apollo 12 crew had reported seeing in flight. The phrase does not originate with the crew in this recording; the flight surgeon is reading it back to them from a paragraph he has "in here," asking them to explain it in their own words. What follows is that explanation, at a level of physiological detail that does not appear anywhere else on this site.

"The first night out, bing, bing"

The crew member responding first - context makes clear this is Pete Conrad, since he goes on to describe his own sleep station and eye - explains that the report reached him indirectly, after the flight:

"We reported it on uh somebody reported it on one of the earlier flights and uh. Some guy up, I got the letter, the paperwork somewhere, and they shipped it down to me at the Cape about two things where the VHF radio noises, which could be discharges from someplace in space, and the other one where, my father was on flight, he's got airport seeing flashes in a spacecraft at night with their eyes closed or with their eyes closed. They, they, they noticed them when they were awake at night and the spacecraft was dark, and they thought they were in a spacecraft."

Read past the transcript's rough edges (this is a Whisper-generated automatic transcript of spoken conversation, not a polished NASA stenographic record, and some words are clearly mis-transcribed - "my father was on flight, he's got airport" almost certainly represents a garbled rendering of a name or unit, not a literal claim about the speaker's father). The substance is clear: a written report from an earlier flight had already flagged two distinct phenomena - VHF radio noise possibly caused by "discharges from someplace in space," and light flashes seen by astronauts with their eyes closed in a dark, sleeping spacecraft. NASA had already circulated a technical explanation to Conrad before this debriefing:

"Yeah. But, uh, whoever this guy was said no, that's a perfectly known phenomenon you can get discharged across your eye from gamma rays or something."

Conrad then describes the in-flight instructions that came with that explanation - specifically, an experiment NASA wanted the crew to run in orbit:

"So he had a bunch of things, would we. Close our eyes and face the moon, you know, when we were orbiting it because it would be more, you'd get less discharges facing the moon than away from it. You don't have to be anywhere near the moon or anywhere. The first night out, bing, bing."

That last line - "the first night out, bing, bing" - is Conrad's own shorthand for how immediately the phenomenon began: not something that required lunar proximity, but something the crew noticed on the very first night of the flight, while still far from the Moon. It directly undercuts the premise NASA had asked the crew to test (that facing the Moon versus facing away from it would change the discharge rate), since the flashes were already happening regardless of orientation before the spacecraft was anywhere near lunar distance.

One eye, not two: the physiological detail NASA was after

The most technically specific portion of the debrief is Conrad's description of exactly how the phenomenon presented, which he offers unprompted and in detail:

"And if you, if you keep your eyes closed and you're awake down there and it's dark and you think about it, when it happens, you can put your eye, you can, you can sit down and it's one eye. It, it's not a discharge that you see in both eyes. You really see it with one eye or the other eye, and you can see what kind it is. We had two kinds."

He then describes the two distinct visual forms the crew catalogued:

"Gets the particle either strike the eyeball perpendicular or or it discharges parallel and we'd get either a a flash like that or we'd get a streak."

This is a crew member proposing his own working physical model in real time - that a particle striking the eye perpendicular to its surface produces a point-like flash, while one passing parallel to the retina produces an elongated streak. Conrad goes on to note an asymmetry specific to himself:

"And uh and I'm the only people that I know is I got. I And I for some reason got it more with my left eye than my right eye, but I could tell which eye it was when I was concentrating on it."

Someone else in the room - the transcript does not make the speaker unambiguous, but the content (a comment about which side of the spacecraft someone slept on) reads as a crew member comparing notes with Conrad - connects that left-eye asymmetry to sleeping position:

"I'm trying to tell you the reason he did it because his, where he slept was always on the left side of the spacecraft. That I got switched on the crosser and I caught all the right ones."

The implication, read plainly: whichever eye faced outward (away from the spacecraft's interior, toward the hull and, by extension, toward space) was the eye that "caught" more of the discharges - which is consistent with a directional source of particles passing through the spacecraft structure rather than a phenomenon generated inside the crew's own bodies.

The flight surgeon connects it to Apollo 11

Partway through the debrief, the flight surgeon volunteers that this is not new to NASA, and that the Apollo 11 crew had reported the identical phenomenon:

"Oh, there's a paper right now on the very exact thing, a new phenomenon that came out of a letter. Buzz and Neil talked about this. I, I remember their conversation about it. They thought it was something, you know, penetrating the spacecraft."

That is a direct, named reference to Buzz Aldrin and Neil Armstrong discussing the same phenomenon after Apollo 11 - and it matters for how this file fits into the broader record. This site's own Apollo 11 Technical Crew Debriefing page documents that the Apollo 11 file mentions cabin light flashes at page 6-37 of the debriefing volume, but notes that "the specific text was cut off in the extraction process" - the underlying words were never recovered from that file. NASA-UAP-D008 does not fill that specific gap (it is a different mission's debrief, not a repaired extraction of D004), but it independently confirms, in the flight surgeon's own words, that Aldrin and Armstrong's Apollo 11 account existed, was documented in a letter, and was being actively discussed by NASA medical staff between missions - and that their working theory at the time was that "something" was "penetrating the spacecraft."

One of the Apollo 12 crew members (context suggests Alan Bean, based on the reference to Al being addressed and described later in the same exchange) then confirms the phenomenon matched exactly across both crews:

"What Pete described is the exact phenomenon that all three of us saw. You'd see a light, you know, just a flash out there or sometimes it'd be a a streak. And I saw them in both eyes, either eye, and, uh, I can't remember if there was one that was more predominant than the other, but I agree with Pete. It's a phenomenon that you're not seeing with both eyes. It's not something external to you. It's something that's inside or coming across you."

The flight surgeon adds NASA's own framing of the phenomenon's status at the time: "Apparently they know about it. Apparently it's a known known phenomenon."

NASA's Mercury-era retina photography experiments

The most historically interesting exchange in the file is the flight surgeon explaining what NASA had already tried, and failed, to do about this phenomenon - going back to Project Mercury, seven years before Apollo 12:

"Well, we've been looking for cosmic rays, you know, cosmic ray strikes and heavy, heavy particles, heavy nuclei that are that go through. You know, we started at clear back in Mercury taking photographs of the retina to see if you could see the streaks across the retina and stuff. We never could find any in there. And so we stopped that up early in the, well by the end of the."

This is a NASA flight surgeon telling three astronauts, on the record, that the space agency had run an actual physical experiment - photographing astronaut retinas - specifically to try to capture visual evidence of the phenomenon, as far back as the Mercury program (1959-1963), and that the experiment produced no positive result. The stated reason the experiment failed is explained immediately afterward, and it is a detail with real diagnostic value for anyone trying to understand why this phenomenon has historically resisted objective confirmation:

"Your eye is what, the fastest healer on your body, and if it's doing anything, you're never going to see it. By the time you get a guy back, all they're ever going to see it is if you photographed it right after a particle went by."

In other words: even if a heavy cosmic-ray particle does leave a transient physical mark on retinal tissue, the eye heals fast enough that by the time an astronaut returns to Earth and can be examined, any such mark would already be gone. That is offered as the working explanation for why the Mercury-era photography experiment came back empty, not as a reason to doubt the phenomenon itself.

Comparing notes: horizontal streaks, one predominant position, a "pinpoint of light"

The back half of the debrief is a detailed, almost clinical, cross-comparison between crew members on the specific geometry of what they saw - the flight surgeon is visibly trying to extract a consistent, testable pattern:

"Were, were these random, uh, distribution of these, uh, or, or did they seem to be fairly horizontal? OK, uh, were these, were these things random or did they all seem to be in one direction? Like were they always horizontal or were they at random?"

One crew member answers with a specific, repeatable observation:

"Mine, it seemed to me I had the impression that they were always roughly in the same place and mine were horizontal. The horizontal streaks were always, if I knew where I was looking, it always seemed like the horizontal streak was a little bit above the center and it always seemed to me it went from right to left. No matter which eye it was."

The same speaker distinguishes the streaks from a separate class of discharge, describing it as a fixed point rather than a moving line:

"The, uh, the, uh, discharges were more towards the center and, and it would be just a, it was just like a pinpoint of light."

Another crew member, asked to confirm, offers his own angle estimate: "What's that about 30 degrees of horizontal, but they were kind of, see, I didn't notice them from the right or left." A third comparison follows: "The horizontal ones that I saw were generally above eye level was generally high." This is three trained observers, independently, converging on a consistent geometric description - flashes and horizontal streaks positioned above center in the visual field - of a phenomenon none of them could see with their eyes open and none of them could point to an external light source for.

One of the astronauts closes out the exchange with a small, human detail about what it was actually like to try to observe the phenomenon deliberately, describing killing time in his sleeping bag: "It wouldn't make any difference whether my eyes were open or closed. I could lay there for, I used to kill time that way, say for a half an hour. Either open them or close them and wait, and sure enough, after a while, you'd get one. And then I'd sit there for a while and say, OK, was it in the left eye or the right eye? It'd take you a while to sort it out, and, uh, but you can usually figure out which eye it was in." The flight surgeon's dry reply captures the domestic strangeness of it: "Now you know what you're doing when you're supposed to be sleeping."

What this file does NOT establish

  • This is not a claim of an externally sourced UFO or unidentified craft. Every crew member in the recording describes the phenomenon as internal to their own vision - "it's not something external to you," in Bean's words - not an object outside the spacecraft.
  • NASA's own working explanation, articulated by the flight surgeon in this file, is a "perfectly known phenomenon" caused by radiation (gamma rays or cosmic ray/heavy-nuclei strikes) interacting with the eye. The crew does not dispute this explanation; they engage with it and add their own observational detail to it.
  • The Mercury-era retina photography experiment did not find physical evidence of the phenomenon on film, and the file offers a specific reason (rapid ocular healing) for why that negative result does not disprove the phenomenon.
  • This file does not resolve the VHF radio noise question Conrad mentions at the very start ("which could be discharges from someplace in space") - that thread is raised once, in the opening minute, and not returned to for the remainder of the recording as transcribed.
  • The transcript on this page is a machine (Whisper) transcription of spoken audio, not an official NASA stenographic transcript. Word-level accuracy on some phrases (proper nouns, background noise, overlapping speech) cannot be guaranteed at the same level as a typed document, which is why this page flags ambiguous speaker attribution explicitly rather than asserting it.
